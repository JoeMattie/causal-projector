<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7fb6-99de-855e17799ec8" -->
The first home-care visit ended at the bus stop.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7fc2-81e7-76fb90fa50c1" -->
The aide arrived Tuesday at eight carrying an agency badge, a canvas tote, and a folder of activities Ruth did not request. At 8:31 she called Evan at work. Ruth had taken the spiral-bound algebra workbook from the library sale, put on her coat, and left through the side gate while the aide was making tea.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7b10-a6a3-3336b5d26742" -->
Evan found her three blocks away beneath the number-seven bus sign. The workbook was open on her knees. She had written three systems of equations on the back of the agency schedule and was waiting for a bus that had not served the community college in eleven years.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-73f1-8575-a51b2c003a30" -->
"My class starts at ten," she said when he approached.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7b2f-b249-c0bd8469c513" -->
"You don't teach there anymore."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7bcc-8887-23b951c4a2c8" -->
Ruth looked at the workbook, then at the timetable fixed behind scratched plastic. The conviction left her face without being replaced by understanding.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-79d9-989e-f264ea6097d8" -->
"Then I've missed something," she said.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-75f7-a8ca-0e0ec82dac96" -->
The aide had followed at a distance and was speaking to her supervisor. The agency called the event unsupervised exit, although the aide had been in the house and Ruth had known exactly how to leave it. By noon, Denise had sent Evan a form recommending a door alarm and reassessment after thirty days.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7055-b20d-4278da386bbf" -->
Ruth read the form twice at the kitchen table. The second time she understood what it proposed.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7d49-bb5e-76ad8ee43599" -->
"They want the house to report me," she said.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7792-b680-a66f053e471a" -->
"They want an alarm on the side door."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7f01-816e-ff25caff2c17" -->
"That is what I said."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-775a-a849-6df503af74ea" -->
She pushed the form away and noticed the note she had left beneath it on Saturday: ASK EVAN WHY HIS MACHINE MAKES PEOPLE BETTER.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7adb-902b-71c97977546a" -->
"Did I ask you?"

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-724d-9411-67eac17fbcdb" -->
"No."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7162-bd3e-512189dfda4e" -->
"Then I'm asking."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-70aa-a536-c4b877a62c6b" -->
Evan explained the shortest version that did not become a lie. The apparatus produced a temporary state in which competing possibilities lost force. He and Lena had both solved problems faster during it. They did not know what the machine removed, whether anything was damaged, or why decisions inside its feedback loop changed physical statistics. Lena believed further human exposure was unsafe.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-745e-8e34-8638faee114e" -->
Ruth listened without interrupting. When he finished, she pointed toward the garage.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-76ae-a610-24f5a8233fdd" -->
"You think it might make the missing parts line up."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7813-8aaa-af699bd42764" -->
"I think the effect has something to do with how information stays consistent. That isn't a treatment."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-71a2-9b37-4e193c102dab" -->
"Neither is a lock on the door."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7f2c-b061-b9a6123e6a83" -->
He told her the joint hold prohibited exposing patients and dependents. Ruth asked who had classified her as either. He said Lena had written the language because they lacked safety data.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-73ed-8533-5b33916ee9de" -->
"Call her," Ruth said.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-76fd-80be-d474d8257c35" -->
Lena answered on the third ring. Evan described the bus stop, Ruth's request, and a reduced protocol he had not yet designed. Lena said no before he reached field strength.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-736a-86e0-0be468496f30" -->
"You cannot consent her into a risk neither of you can describe," she said.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7306-b000-2ff1149a94d5" -->
Ruth took the phone. "I understand that he doesn't know what will happen."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7027-a460-b799ae42910d" -->
Lena's voice became quieter. "That is part of it. We also don't know whether the person who comes out would be the person making this choice."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-758f-8133-d9d960f5e7c5" -->
Ruth looked at Evan. "That happens to me without the machine."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7626-b5d8-1b1fc870bff4" -->
Lena asked to speak to him again. She said the hold remained. A neutral replication site was being discussed. If he broke the seal, she would not pretend it was a protocol deviation.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7fb0-8748-4abc425e3e2c" -->
"What would you call it?" he asked.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7eca-bc86-a42959d5d049" -->
"An experiment on your mother."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-760b-96a1-9dddb38f5db9" -->
After the call, Evan told Ruth they would not run the machine that day. Ruth said he had asked the person who told him not to do it and received the expected answer. This was responsible, but it had not answered her.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-714e-949c-22ef19cc37d2" -->
"I want six minutes," she said. "No memories picked in advance. No questions about your father. If I say stop, it stops without you deciding whether I mean it."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7a2c-ae67-2a4b7aa52582" -->
"You might not remember agreeing."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7719-878d-88e65c60a4a8" -->
"Record me. You record the refrigerator."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7fef-ab6b-6cdfce5e59d1" -->
He recorded the conversation. On camera, Ruth described the unknowns in her own words. The machine might do nothing, might make her thinking clearer briefly, might produce a convincing error, or might leave a change neither of them could reverse. She stated that Lena opposed the attempt and that Evan was her son, caregiver, and the machine's builder.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-75cb-996b-d50e030d1f16" -->
"Do you think I should do it?" Evan asked.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7fe8-a80a-640e94ce482f" -->
"I think you want to," Ruth said. "That is why I am giving the instructions."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7673-bec2-5c5695ceb16c" -->
He should have treated the sentence as a stop condition. Instead he treated it as evidence of capacity.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-789f-8770-30c9d88d41e1" -->
Evan spent the afternoon building RUTH R-01. He reduced RF power to twenty percent, limited the anomalous phase walk to six minutes, and placed Ruth at the kitchen table rather than near the apparatus. Her answers traveled over a one-way audio classifier and a large three-button pad. The red button cut the RF contactor directly. It did not pass through his software.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7e79-ad64-e8187d2be852" -->
The feedback score used only present-tense tasks: identify a displayed object, compare two quantities, hold a three-item sequence, choose whether a statement followed from the previous one. No autobiographical answer was rewarded. Stable performance selected CONTINUE; an error, nonresponse, or stop input selected WAIT. The apparatus could favor coherence, but it had no name or childhood to aim at.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7bff-a5d7-def2ea0aa5ff" -->
```text
RUTH R-01: REDUCED CLOSED LOOP

participant-requested duration: 6 min maximum
RF power: 20% of OPERATOR L-01
feedback: present-tense task stability only
autobiographical target: none

participant controls:
  red switch -> hardware RF disconnect
  spoken STOP -> independent classifier disconnect

abort:
  any distress, two errors, nonresponse, gradient limit
```

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7880-9f5c-75dcc2fd83b1" -->
At 6:10, Evan showed Ruth the numbered seal on the garage disconnect. She confirmed that breaking it violated his agreement with Lena. Then she cut the red cable tie herself with kitchen scissors and handed both pieces to him.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-729b-b2fd-86c62526027e" -->
"Now neither of us can pretend you tricked me," she said.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-79ed-b1ea-02730b428ddf" -->
The statement did not make the choice equal. Evan knew how the apparatus worked, controlled the description of its risks, and wanted the result. Ruth knew what it felt like to become less able to protect a choice from other people's concern. Neither fact canceled the other.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-70b5-bed6-b5f00408b9cd" -->
He armed the safety task. Ruth sat at the kitchen table with the button pad beneath her right hand. Denise's pen, the algebra workbook, and the home-care form had been moved out of view.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7d16-a1c5-542fe5d3a43a" -->
The first two minutes remained ordinary. Ruth named a cup, a key, and a photograph. She reversed three digits, missed the next sequence, and waited while the controller held the phase walk shallow.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-73cf-a8a1-a55736fc3c6f" -->
At 6:13, five sensor channels in the garage began sharing one mode. At the edge of Evan's vision the grain returned, laid over the white of the cupboard door, and it did not clear when he looked at it directly. He was in the kitchen. He was not the subject of the run. He made the same decision he had made twice before and left it out of the report.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7a53-8c14-60fe1b5892fa" -->
Ruth looked toward the utility door.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7ee4-82d9-7c1691158db0" -->
"Something stopped," she said.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7614-b9a4-9b817a040956" -->
Evan checked the audio trace. The refrigerator compressor had switched off.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-71c7-aaf9-9e58be6a0b79" -->
"Only the refrigerator."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7e09-bbf8-cd33230e5720" -->
"No," Ruth said. "The crowd."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-702e-a66a-d0948772560b" -->
Her next six answers were correct. She identified the day, date, room, and reason for the test without prompting. She held five items in order, rejected a syllogism with a missing premise, and complained that the object-identification set treated a mug and a cup as different categories without specifying function.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-720f-9a71-a0a5ef741dd8" -->
The automatic stop opened the contactor at six minutes. Ruth did not touch the red button. Evan entered the kitchen only after the RF current reached zero.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-72bb-90dd-49e74d2ecc94" -->
She looked at him with an attention that did not slide away.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7c63-b970-b465ff71c9e1" -->
"You need a haircut," she said.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7cac-86d7-67587d58ebd0" -->
He laughed before he could prevent it. Ruth laughed because he had. The exchange was so ordinary that he had to sit down.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7b51-9fd3-8a202c9ec67f" -->
For the next twenty minutes she remained clear. She knew her husband had died four years earlier. She remembered planning the funeral and being angry that the church had changed the time without asking. She knew why the aide had come, why she had gone to the bus stop, and why a door alarm felt like being demoted inside her own house.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7ecf-907b-c22399eb4292" -->
"I was going to teach," she said. "It was real until I saw the timetable. Then I knew something was wrong, but I couldn't find which side of the mistake I was on."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7510-9073-87725d4e5be2" -->
She asked Evan to write this down. She did not want the agency told about the machine. She wanted the morning visits to continue, the side gate left usable, and a card in her coat explaining that the number-seven bus no longer reached the college.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-78cf-b089-72b2e193fb5f" -->
"An alarm is information for you," she said. "The card is information for me."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7076-bbb5-87d48aaf830d" -->
Evan wrote it exactly.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7a4b-905d-0938b78b864f" -->
At 6:41, Ruth asked whether Celia had returned his call.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7966-a079-11d655e8cbbd" -->
Evan thought he had missed part of the conversation. "Who?"

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7959-8f8f-a2bb8de1f663" -->
"Your sister."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7121-95ef-e0512124f7f7" -->
Ruth said it without hesitation. Celia was three years older than Evan. She lived near Tacoma, taught chemistry at a secondary school, and had a daughter named Nora who was studying nursing. Celia had broken her left wrist falling from the apricot tree behind their first house. She hated yellow flowers because Ruth had filled the hospital room with daffodils.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7559-b40a-9f1c09ee2669" -->
Evan had no sister.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7345-9de3-6a284dd47130" -->
He asked the question twice in different forms. Ruth's account did not drift. Celia had missed their father's funeral because a storm canceled the last flight south. She had called from the airport while Evan stood outside the church, angry at her for not leaving earlier. Ruth remembered telling him that grief did not improve airline schedules.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7f34-84fd-8e54ebb66a17" -->
She remembered Evan's eulogy. She quoted the first sentence correctly.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7f88-bda7-1c4732cb184b" -->
"There was no Celia," he said.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7cb5-9e2c-55a72436d56f" -->
Ruth's face changed, but not into confusion. "Don't do that."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-75ff-a7da-f9d167b9817c" -->
"Mom, you had one child. Me."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7a36-ab36-e582d5092602" -->
"I know who you are. I also know who she is."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-73d1-947f-7758a502a767" -->
She went to the hall cabinet and removed the family album without asking where it was. Her hands shook, but she turned to the correct decade. The photographs showed Ruth and her husband before Evan, Ruth holding Evan as a baby, Evan at five beside a plastic paddling pool. No girl stood between them.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7114-9393-5c7d8f6869d5" -->
Ruth touched an empty pocket in the album's plastic page.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-72d2-8a2a-26e53dc0e153" -->
"Her school photograph was here."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-71f6-a899-0ab7c0401ecd" -->
The pocket held only a pale rectangle where paper had protected the backing from age. Evan could not remember what photograph had occupied it. Empty pockets were common in the album. Ruth had moved pictures into frames, mailed them to relatives, and reorganized pages according to systems no one else could reconstruct.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-73fc-9fe2-e6aadebc444e" -->
None of that explained why the absence had the exact dimensions of the photograph Ruth described.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7bbe-9998-d5b4391462cb" -->
At 7:02 the clarity began to loosen. Ruth repeated the question about Celia's call. She lost the date, recovered it from the consent card, and became angry when Evan tried another orientation question.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7872-aa96-76d6630fd53e" -->
"Stop examining me," she said. "You already got your result."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7d22-93cf-17a22d026ce1" -->
He stopped.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7f87-b731-85b940e86589" -->
Ruth closed the album. "You keep telling me there wasn't a Celia. I am telling you I lost her twice today."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-79bd-bab2-2c49c16345fc" -->
Evan helped her to the sofa because she asked for his arm. He did not correct her again. The improvement had been real. So had the grief.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-77c5-9b20-03443075f64a" -->
After Ruth slept, he opened the run report.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7ed7-864a-1504103e63f2" -->
He stared at the word selected. It converted a person into a result and did not become less accurate when he changed the font.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-77ae-99c1-0691fa294c1c" -->
At 9:14 he sent Lena the consent recording, raw logs, and report.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-76bd-88c4-06264e6683f0" -->

She is safe. The result is not a cure.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-70dd-b111-f6f0793937fb" -->
Lena called before the upload finished.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7558-a1d1-86872098a278" -->
Her first question was whether Ruth was awake, breathing normally, and able to stop the protocol. Her second was whether the apparatus was powered. Evan answered, then waited for the accusation.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7ef7-ba24-fb9ab49ff66e" -->
"Kill the garage breaker," Lena said. "Take the key out of the disconnect. Bring both to Ruth's room."

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7206-a012-42f2ba809bce" -->
He did. On Ruth's bedside table, beside Denise's pen and the note about Tuesday's aide, he placed the breaker lock and the brass key.

<!-- authorbot:block id="019f87f0-62e3-7d5a-a884-3029bfd5b8f8" -->
The family album remained open to a space shaped exactly like someone missing.
